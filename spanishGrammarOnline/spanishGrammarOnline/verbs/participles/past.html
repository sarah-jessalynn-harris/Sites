Uses of the Past Participle

In English, past participles end in “-ed”. There are many irregular past participles in English as well, like written, gotten, drank, and more. It is the same way in Spanish. They have many uses which you can explore below.

As an Adjective

The past participle can be used as an adjective, and changes in gender and number to agree with the noun it is describing.

ex: He is injured.

Está herido.

ex: She is injured.

Está herida.

ex: They are injured.

Están heridos.

ex: The women are injured.

Están heridas.

* When used as an adjective with estar, it is implied that it is a result of another action ¹

ex: He is hurt (because he fell).

Está herido (porque se cayó).

Noun Derived From a Verb

Sometimes, nouns are derived from verbs. Often, these nouns come from the past participle.²

ex: exit

la salida (from salir: to leave)

ex: wounded/injured person

el herido (from herir: to injure)

Passive Voice

Regular Forms

Below you can find out how to form past participles normally. There are some irregularities, and you can find them further down the page.

Forming Regular Past Participles

To form a past participle, you drop the ending of the infinitive and add the apropriate endings. For -ar verbs, you drop the -ar ending and add -ado. For -er and -ir verbs, you drop the -er or -ir ending and add -ido.

Irregular Forms

Present participles can be irregular, too. They either follow the normal pattern, need accents, or they are completely different words. You can find some examples below.

Accents

Verbs with -aer, -eer, and -oir endings will have an accent on the i in the past participle ending -ido.

caer
caído
oír
oído
leer
leído
traer
traído

Irregular Past Participles

Just as in English, there are simply many irregular past participles in Spanish. Somtimes certain verbs have two forms of a past participle, one for verb use and one for adjective use. A few examples are listed below.
